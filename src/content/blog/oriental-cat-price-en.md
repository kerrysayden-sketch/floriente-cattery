"How much is the kitten?" — the most common question. 90% of breeders answer: "DM us." We answer here.

Oriental kittens from a registered cattery cost on average:

- 800–1,500 EUR — pet class
- 1,600–2,500 EUR — breed class
- 2,600–4,500 EUR — show class

Price is influenced by color, pedigree, the kitten's sex, timing, and market supply and demand. But none of these factors are hidden — everything is transparent and explained.

This article lays it all out. When a breeder hides the price — ask yourself what else they might be hiding.

## Price Categories: What They Are and Why They Exist

Kittens from the same litter can have different prices. That's not haggling — it's a classification system.

Pet class (800–1,500 EUR): Kittens with great health and character that don't fully match the show standard. They look like Orientals, act like Orientals, and become strong companions. The differences are in fine details of type (ear set, chin line, etc.) that most people don't notice.

Breed class (1,600–2,500 EUR): Kittens that match the standard and can be used for breeding in a registered cattery. This is a different category of responsibility: it's not just looks being assessed, but genetic potential.

Show class (2,600–4,500 EUR): Kittens with the closest match to the standard and high show potential. Rare. Often reserved in advance. These are the cats that compete in shows and chase titles.

Class is determined by the breeder — not the buyer — after evaluation at 10–12 weeks. The class assigned doesn't make a kitten "better" or "worse" — it shows what the kitten is best suited for.

## The Real Economics of Breeding

Buyers sometimes assume there's a big margin built into the price. The actual math is different.

Raising one litter — from kittens being born to handover — costs around 2,000 EUR under normal conditions. That's food for the growing litter, litter substrate, deworming courses, multiple clinic visits for each vaccination, microchipping, paperwork, and many hours of daily work that can't be fully accounted for in the costs.

As Elvira puts it: "One kitten basically covers the cost of the whole litter." That's true for baseline costs — food, medicine, care.

But when it comes to developing the cattery — outdoor enclosures, houses, show participation, education, genetic programs — those costs only grow and don't get covered within a single litter.

This isn't a classic business model. It's the structural economics of responsible breeding: part of the cost is covered by kitten sales, but a significant share is invested separately.

Genetic testing is one of the key areas. Breeding cats are required to be tested for hereditary diseases.

Approximately:

- FeLV + FIV + FCoV (PCR) — about $90–100
- PK deficiency — about $50–60
- PRA-rdAc — about $50–60

For export abroad, a rabies antibody titer test is performed (about 100 EUR per animal). For pair selection and litter planning — genetic compatibility tests (also about 100 EUR per animal). Genetic color tests are run separately.

Both breeding cats undergo baseline testing before producing a litter. These costs aren't visible in the price of a specific kitten — but they're what builds the invisible infrastructure of responsible breeding.

## Color Affects Price

Not all colors are equally common in the Oriental breed. Rarity has value — and that's reflected in price.

Standard tier: Spotted tabbies and common solid colors. Most frequent in the breed and represent the lower end of the pet class range.

Premium tier: Blue, lavender, cinnamon, chocolate, tortoiseshell. These colors require specific genetic combinations from both parents — they don't appear by chance. A lavender Oriental or a cinnamon is the result of conscious breeding decisions made one or two generations ago.

The Oriental breed is one of the few that contains all of these colors within a single standard. If you have a specific dream color — availability depends on the current litter, not on the breeder's catalog. Rare colors are often reserved before birth.

## Hidden Costs After Purchase

The kitten price is one-time. The cost of ownership is ongoing. Before budgeting, here's what the first year typically takes in Ukraine:

| Recurring expenses | Approximate range (UAH / ≈ EUR) |
| --- | --- |
| Premium food (wet + dry) | 3,000–8,000 UAH / month (~60–155 EUR) |
| Annual vet check + boosters | 3,000–6,000 UAH / year (~60–115 EUR) |
| Pet insurance | Varies by country — optional cost item |
| Quality scratching post | 5,000–10,000 UAH (one-time) (~100–195 EUR), or handmade — even cheaper |
| Toys and environmental enrichment | 200–1,000 UAH / month (~4–20 EUR) |
| Litter and supplies | 500–1,500 UAH / month (~10–30 EUR) |

Elvira on the price range for scratching posts: "You can spend 120–130 EUR on a scratching post, or 600–650 EUR — it all depends on what you can afford and what you want."

The point is — quality enrichment for a hyperactive breed is a real budget line, not a small detail.

Orientals aren't self-sufficient cats. They need stimulation. The cost of insufficient enrichment for an Oriental shows up later — in behavioral problems and vet bills.

A separate budget item is activity infrastructure. Beyond the basics (bowls, litter boxes, scratching post), an Oriental benefits from an active environment: tunnels, climbing shelves, puzzle toys, sleeping cocoons. Some owners add an exercise wheel (like a treadmill for cats) — especially useful for young animals. An outdoor enclosure for safe balcony or yard access is a separate investment (starting from a few hundred euros for installation). This isn't luxury: without an active environment, an Oriental starts to suffer — gets sick from stress or "flips" things in the house. Especially critical for kittens during growth.

## "Cheap from a Listing" vs. a Responsible Breeder — What You Actually Get

This is an uncomfortable comparison. But necessary.

From time to time, Oriental kittens show up on classifieds for 200–400 EUR from private sellers. Here's what that price typically doesn't include:

- WCF pedigree or any verified lineage
- Genetic testing of parents for PRA
- Full vaccination protocol
- Microchip at handover
- Any post-sale support
- Show results or verified cattery registration data

Elvira on the difference: "It's not just someone who set up a page on OLX, bought two cats, and is selling kittens" — it's not casual activity, it's systematic work.

Professional input is knowledge and experience built up over years. Veterinary medicine, genetics, selection, raising and socializing the animals — all of it takes continuous learning. This isn't a hobby, it's qualified work.

The risk of a cheap purchase isn't only financial. An untested breeding pair can produce kittens with hereditary pathologies. PRA (progressive retinal atrophy) in Orientals can lead to blindness. A kitten without complete vaccination documentation will cost you more on the first vet visit alone than the price difference you saved.

## FAQ

**Why does an Oriental kitten cost 800–1,500 EUR?**

The price reflects real costs: genetic testing of both parents, three rounds of vaccinations, microchipping, pedigree registration, multiple vet visits, food and supplies from birth, and the professional infrastructure of a licensed cattery. Raising a litter costs about 2,000 EUR before a single kitten is sold. The price isn't arbitrary.

**Does pricing differ for rare colors?**

Yes. Blue, lavender, cinnamon, chocolate, and tortoiseshell Orientals are priced at the upper range or discussed individually. These colors require specific genetic combinations and appear less often. Standard spotted or common solid colors are the lower range.

**What additional costs should EU buyers expect?**

Added to the kitten price: EU-standard veterinary certificate, transport (train/air depending on route), accompanying paperwork, rabies antibody titer test, and possibly a quarantine fee depending on the country. These items aren't included in the base kitten price — arranged individually before the contract is signed.

**Can the price be negotiated?**

The price reflects real costs, not a negotiation buffer. We don't inflate prices to discount them later. What you see is the real number. Because transparency is the point.

**What's the difference between pet, breed, and show classes?**

Pet class: ideal companion, doesn't meet the strict show standard. Breed class: meets the standard, suitable for registered breeding programs. Show class: exceptional conformation, ready for shows. Class is determined by the breeder at 10–12 weeks (2.5–3 months) based on evaluation against the standard of a recognized registry (WCF, FIFe, TICA, etc.).

**What's the deposit and is it refundable?**

Reservation deposit — 500 EUR. The deposit is non-refundable if the buyer cancels, but may be refunded under conditions written into the contract (for example, force majeure or kitten health issues). The remaining balance is paid at handover. Specific terms are agreed when the contract is signed.

**Is the kitten sterilized before handover?**

Pet class kittens are handed over already sterilized — the procedure is done by our vet. How it works: once the buyer is confirmed (payment made), after 3 months of age our vet takes the kitten in for sterilization. Then 2 weeks of recovery at our home. Only after that does the kitten go to the new family. So the minimum handover age for a pet kitten is around 3.5 months. Show kittens aren't sterilized right away: we usually keep them until a registered buyer in another region (to avoid creating local competition). We don't transfer breeding cats to pet clients.

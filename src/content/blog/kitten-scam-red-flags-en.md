While I was looking for Halva, scammers tried to fool me five times. Here's how I learned to tell a breeder from a scammer.

Online pet sale scams aren't a small inconvenience. According to EU consumer protection data, around 50% of buyers who purchased animals online in 2024 ran into fraudulent listings. Purebred kittens are one of the top targets. The schemes are organized, adaptive, and emotionally calibrated to hit at the most vulnerable moment: when you've already fallen for the photo.

Short answer: Three schemes cover most kitten scams. Seven verification points filter out 95% of fake listings.

## The Three Schemes You'll Run Into

### Scheme 1: Phishing on OLX and Facebook

Scammers create fake Facebook groups and OLX listings using stolen photos — often square Instagram crops from real catteries. Elvira ran into this herself when she was looking for her first cat: she'd find listings from the US using photos from real catteries she actually knew. She'd write to those breeders: "Someone else is using your photos, is this you?" Reply — "No, not us." Then — "I tried to buy your cat in the US." "No, that's not us." That's how the same photos circulate across dozens of fake listings.

The contact pattern is consistent: the seller messages first via Viber or Telegram, the conversation moves fast, and at payment time an "OLX Delivery" link appears — a phishing page collecting card details or a deposit that vanishes.

The declared location is usually the US or Canada. That's purely a stalling tactic: "the kitten's on the way," "at customs," "in transit." Elvira on this directly: "Basically these are con artists who'll just take the money and never send you a cat." They take the money. There is no cat.

### Scheme 2: Double-Sale Fraud

A more sophisticated version. The seller prepares a legally drafted contract — sometimes through a real lawyer — gets a deposit or full payment, and then sends a different person to pick the kitten up. The "buyer on paper" then approaches the breeder demanding their money back, claiming they never received the kitten.

This exact situation happened to a club colleague of Elvira's. The practical takeaway: if handover is in person — the person who signed the contract and the person physically picking up the kitten must be one and the same. ID-with-photo verification at handover isn't optional. If handover is via courier — that physical check isn't possible, so heightened attention to the contract itself and to identifying the recipient on the other end of the route. This double-sale scheme most often works on international deals with multi-party contracts — less often on domestic handovers.

As Oleksandr noted: "You agree on one thing, then the scheme suddenly changes." — You agree on one thing. Then the terms shift.

### Scheme 3: The Ghost Seller and Invented Costs

The kitten doesn't exist. What exists is a sequence of invented payments, each demanded before the previous "obstacle" can be "resolved": EU-standard oxygen container (~200 EUR), customs clearance, vet certificate at the border. Each payment buys a new delay and a new demand. The process continues until the buyer stops paying or recognizes the scheme.

The tell: every question from the seller comes back to money. A real breeder's questions come back to the kitten — where it'll live, who'll be looking after it, are there kids or other pets. "If a scammer's selling, what matters to him is getting the money. And basically all his questions come back to money."

## 7-Point Verification Checklist

Run this checklist before any deposit. Each item is binary: passed or failed.

| # | Item | Passed | Failed |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Breeder identity | Full name, cattery name, physical address | "Message us," location given as country only |
| 2 | Registry check | WCF, TICA, or CFA registration — found online by cattery name | Registration number not provided |
| 3 | Parent documents | Pedigrees of both parents, health test results, photos with names | Only the kitten shown; parents undocumented |
| 4 | Reverse image search | Photos exist only on this breeder's platforms | Same photos in multiple unrelated listings |
| 5 | Live interactive video call | Kitten and mother together in frame, unscripted tasks performed in real time | Recording only, video call refused, static background |
| 6 | Written contract | Contract includes buyer, seller, kitten details, handover terms | No contract offered or terms shift between conversations |
| 7 | Secure payment | Bank transfer to a named account, confirmed receipt | Payment link via messenger, crypto, "OLX Delivery" link |

A real breeder isn't surprised by any of these items. Most of this they'll provide before you ask.

## Anti-Deepfake Video Call

A video call is necessary but not sufficient. A scammer can show pre-recorded footage, borrow a kitten for one session, or hold a photo up to the camera. The call has to be structured so those workarounds become impossible.

What a valid video call must include:
- Kitten and mother in the same frame. A breeder without the kitten can't show the mother.
- A real-time task with a written note. Before the call, send the message: "Please show a sheet of paper with my name and today's date." That's impossible to fake with recorded footage and neutralizes deepfake overlays.
- A walk through the room. Ask the breeder to walk through the space, showing the cattery environment. A static background — especially one wall with no depth — is a warning signal.
- Questions about the breed. "A real cat person — they only ever talk about cats." A genuine cat person can't stop. Ask about the kitten's eating habits, play preferences, reaction to noise. A scammer has no answers. A breeder doesn't have time to tell you everything.

If the seller refuses any part of this call structure — the conversation is over.

## What a Real Breeder Does

The behavioral difference between a scammer and a verified breeder is consistent across every parameter.

| Parameter | Scammer Practice | Floriente Practice |

The main test is simple. A scammer wants your money. A breeder wants to know where their kitten is going. These are different conversations from the first message.

## FAQ

**How do I check WCF cattery registration?**

Go to the WCF site (wcf-online.de) — or the site of any other recognized registry (FIFe, TICA, CFA) — and search for the cattery by name or registration number. An official cattery has a profile that comes up in search. If the name returns nothing and the seller can't explain why — treat them as unregistered.

Why does this matter at all? Club registration isn't just paperwork. It means the club has verified: the medical condition of breeding cats, the breeding permit for a specific pair (no familial relation), conformity to the breed standard, blood purity. An untested pair can produce a kitten that looks healthy but has serious hidden problems: improper crosses (even within one breed) lead to bad joints, heart, immune system. Example from another breed: Scottish Fold can't be crossed with another Scottish Fold — only with British Shorthair (upright ears × folded). Fold × Fold crosses produce kittens with serious lifelong musculoskeletal issues. Club registration is protection against that math when sellers just want to make a quick buck.

**What do I do if I find stolen photos in someone else's listing?**

Run a reverse image search (Google Images or TinEye). If those same photos are on another breeder's site or social media — contact them directly. They should know their photos are being used and can confirm whether there's any connection to that listing.

**Is a separate fee for the pedigree ever justified?**

There are two types of pedigree to distinguish. The PARENTS' pedigree — the breeder is required to show it free of charge, no fees attached. It may have registration numbers partly redacted to protect against copying — that's normal and acceptable. If the seller demands ~200 EUR just to show the parent pedigrees — that's a documented scam, don't fall for it. The KITTEN's own pedigree is a separate document ordered through the club and does cost extra. It's billed on top of the base kitten price if you need it. The metrika (birth certificate) is always included in the price.

**What if the seller is abroad and says they can't do a video call?**

Legitimate international breeders agree to video calls — the time difference is minor compared to what they're asking you to pay. A seller who keeps citing technical problems is using that as cover. One failed call is a practical problem. A pattern is a red flag.

**How do I verify the identity of the person picking up the kitten (if I'm the breeder)?**

Require a government photo ID at handover. The name on the ID has to match the name on the contract. A different person picking up the kitten — regardless of explanation — voids the agreed terms and sets up the conditions for a double-sale scam.

**Should I be worried that a breeder asks a lot of questions about my lifestyle?**

No. That's the right behavior. A breeder asking about your apartment size, daily routine, other pets, and plans for the cat is doing exactly what a responsible breeder should be doing. The seller who isn't curious about your situation and just wants payment confirmation — that's the one to question.

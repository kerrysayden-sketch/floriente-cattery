You don't choose a kitten. You find yours. Sometimes the breeder sees the match before you do.

You came to look at photos. It ended in a 45-minute conversation about your work schedule, the size of your apartment, and whether you've ever had a cat at all. This isn't small talk — it's the process. A responsible breeder isn't selling you a kitten. They're deciding whether your home is the right place for one.

This article is the ready-made framework: what the classes mean, how to read character from birth, what questions you'll be asked (and why), what a real video call looks like, and the exact 7 items you need to check at handover.

## Pet / Breed / Show — What It Means for You

Every registered kitten is evaluated against the breed standard and falls into one of three categories. The category affects price, breeding rights, and what's expected of you as the owner.

| Category | What It Means | Price Range | Conditions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pet | Healthy, typical character. Possible (but not required) minor deviations from standard. Often — a strategic decision by the breeder with no defects at all. | Lowest | Sterilization required; no breeding rights |
| Breed | Strong fit to standard; suitable for a registered breeding program | Mid to high | Sold exclusively to registered WCF/FIFe/TICA breeders; breeding contract required |
| Show | Exceptional fit to standard — judges will take notice on the show table | Highest | Co-ownership possible; show career expected |

What that means for most buyers: pet class is the right call. An Oriental pet class kitten from a serious cattery comes from genetically tested parents (testing is done on breeding cats, not on each kitten individually), vaccinated, microchipped, and behaviorally healthy. If the buyer wants an additional individual test on the specific kitten — the breeder doesn't refuse: the client pays the test cost, the breeder takes the cat in for sample collection. The "defect" that dropped the kitten from show class is invisible to you in daily life. Health and character — identical.

Important nuance: pet class isn't always about defects. Often the entire litter goes to pet with no faults at all. The reason is market logic: when demand exceeds supply, it makes more sense for the breeder to sell pet (with mandatory sterilization) than to give breeding rights and create a competitor next door. So pet class isn't always "something's off" — often it's a strategic call by the cattery.

If the listing doesn't specify a category — or the breeder doesn't know this classification — that's a signal to pay attention to.

## How Character Reads from Birth

Character assessment doesn't start at 8 weeks. It starts at the first feeding.

Watch the litter at mealtime. One kitten can't find a nipple and fusses. Another shoves siblings out of the way with their paws, takes one spot, moves to another, takes a third. "You can tell that's the cheeky one who'll be the boss cat later." The first kitten to start eating solid food on their own when weaning begins — around week 3 — is usually the litter leader.

Three tests give reliable signals in the 8-to-14-week window (roughly 2–3.5 months):

The approach test. Place the kitten on a neutral surface. Does it explore or freeze? Approach a stranger or hide? Confident kittens explore. Anxious ones flatten themselves to the floor.

The play test. Offer a wand toy. The reaction shows energy level, prey drive, and — importantly — how the kitten recovers from over-arousal. Does it exit play gracefully or escalate?

Hand tolerance. Flip the kitten onto its back. Hold for 30 seconds. This predicts adult tolerance for grooming, vet handling, and kids.

One number anchors the behavioral science here: the study of 5,726 cats (University of Helsinki, 2017) confirmed that early separation from the mother — before the 2–9-week socialization window closes (0.5–2 months) — is a meaningful predictor of aggression, stereotypic behavior, and fear in adulthood. A kitten leaving its mother at 8 weeks (2 months) isn't the same animal as one leaving at 12–14 weeks (3–3.5 months).

The mother's behavior is the single best predictor you have. A calm, social queen raises kittens who trust people. If the breeder won't show you the mother — on video, in person, or at least in several photos — ask why.

## The Buyer Interview — What a Good Breeder Asks You

The first serious question in a conversation with a buyer isn't "which kitten do you want?" It's closer to: "Are you familiar with the Oriental breed? What have you heard? What exactly are you looking for?"

Before recommending anything, a responsible breeder maps out your situation:

- Living conditions — apartment or house, square meters, outdoor access
- Household composition — kids, other pets, number of adults
- Work schedule — how many hours a day nobody's home; Orientals with separation anxiety need a companion animal if left for 8+ hours
- Return plan — what happens if circumstances change in year 3 or year 10? (This is a 15-year commitment)
- Financial readiness — annual vet budget, awareness of breed-specific costs, emergency reserve
- Allergies — Fel d 1 levels in Orientals are below average, but "hypoallergenic" doesn't mean "allergen-free." Six months of correspondence with a buyer who ultimately couldn't tolerate any cat — that's not a failure. That's the process working as intended.

One detail: if you ask for a discount in the first conversation, the breeder hears something specific. It's not about the amount — it's a signal about how the conversation about the vet bill in year two will go.

30% of inquiries don't become sales. That's not a failure rate. That's a filter working as designed.

## What to Expect from a Video Call

In an era of deepfakes and stock-photo scams, a video call isn't optional. It's the minimum proof of existence. The anchor of responsible buying: the kitten is real, the mother is real, the space is real.

A legitimate video call includes:

- Kitten and mother shown together — not in sequence, but in the same frame or at least the same room, with visible interaction
- An unscripted on-camera task — the breeder picks up the kitten during the call without warning; you watch the reaction. A socialized kitten doesn't panic. A kitten that hisses or freezes at unexpected touch has a socialization gap.
- A walk-through of the room — the space where the kittens live. Clean, with environmental enrichment, with access to resources (litter box, water, toys). Multiple levels. Other cats present and calm.

Important to distinguish a cage from a playpen. Adult cats live freely throughout the house — no restrictions. Small kittens often have a "baby corner" — a clear playpen or a special enclosure (cattery aquarium) where they're warm, comfortable, can see the whole house and socialize, but are protected from getting cold or finding dangerous spots. That's not a cage — it's necessary safety for the little ones, and it expands with age and is eventually removed entirely. If you see kittens in a clear enclosure with everything they need on the video call — that's normal. A cage, on the other hand — a small enclosed space without comfort — is a red flag.

- Live answers without a script — a real breeder doesn't need to glance at notes to answer "how does this kitten interact with the others?"

If the seller refuses a video call, claims technical problems, or only offers pre-recorded footage — these aren't minor inconveniences. These are first-tier red flags.

The video call happens before any deposit.

## Handover Documents — 7-Item Checklist for the EU

This is the document checklist for a kitten transferred within the EU or from Ukraine to Europe. Every item on this list exists for a reason. Missing documents aren't administrative oversights. They're a question of responsibility.

| # | Document | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | EU pet passport (within EU) and animal health certificate / AHC (Ukraine to EU) | Required for legal entry |
| 2 | ISO microchip, 15 digits | Must be implanted before travel; number recorded in passport |
| 3 | Vaccination record — core viral panel and rabies | Dates, vaccine name, batch number, vet signature |
| 4 | Rabies titer result (if required by destination country) | Blood test from an accredited lab; result ≥0.5 IU/ml |
| 5 | Sales contract | States class, sterilization clause if applicable, return policy |
| 6 | Metrika (kitten card / birth certificate); pedigree available on request | Standard handover includes the metrika — the official birth document with a club number. The pedigree (3–5 generations) is ordered separately through the club at the owner's initiative and for an additional fee. Issued by the registry (WCF, FIFe, TICA). |
| 7 | Genetic test results for sire and dam | Minimum cardiac screening for HCM; PRA if relevant for the breed |

A kitten without a metrika from a recognized registry isn't a "purebred kitten." It's a kitten with a piece of paper. The metrika is the baseline document confirming origin: it lists the kitten's information and the contact details of the club, where you can verify the data directly and reach out if needed.

A 3–5 generation pedigree is an additional document you can order separately through the club if you need it (more often needed for breeding than for pet class owners).

Hard truth: if a breeder offers a kitten under 12 weeks (3 months) — walk. The kitten isn't ready. Neither is the breeder. It's a direct violation of club and felinology system rules — meaning that kitten isn't club-registered.

## FAQ

**At what age should an Oriental kitten go to a new home?**

Minimum 12 weeks (3 months). Most responsible breeders keep kittens until 14–16 weeks (3.5–4 months). The 2–9-week socialization window (0.5–2 months) needs to close properly — within the litter and with the mother. Early separation — documented in the 5,726-cat study — correlates with aggression and anxiety in adulthood. Anything younger than 12 weeks is an animal welfare issue.

**What's the difference between a "purebred kitten" and a "registered kitten"?**

A registered kitten has a metrika issued by the club — an official birth document with the club number and information about the kitten and its parents. Standard handover includes the metrika. The pedigree (3–5 generations) is a separate document the new owner can order through the club at their own initiative.

"Pedigree" without registration paperwork is a marketing term, not a verified fact. Always check which registry the cattery is registered with, and verify the registration number directly on the system's official site.

**Can I pick the kitten myself, or does the breeder assign one?**

Both happen. Experienced breeders often watch as buyers and kittens find each other naturally — "like the stars line up." The breeder may guide the match based on temperament data collected over weeks 8–14. If you have clear preferences about character (highly active or calm, leader or follower) — say so up front. The breeder has information you don't.

Worried the kitten will get taken while you're thinking? "If it's yours — you'll buy it," Elvira says. The right match has a habit of waiting.

**What questions can I ask the breeder?**

All of them. Ask about genetic testing protocols, socialization methods, vaccination schedule, what document package comes with handover, how many cats currently live in the cattery, and what post-handover support the breeder offers. A breeder who dodges specific questions about health testing isn't being modest — they're being evasive. For the full list, see [18 questions to ask a breeder before buying](/en/blog/questions-to-ask-cat-breeder/).

**Is it normal to wait several months in line?**

Yes, and it's a positive sign. A cattery that always has kittens available either breeds litters too often or has trouble keeping buyers. Waiting 3–6 months for a specific color or class from a serious breeder is standard. Use the time to prepare the home.

Another important question at this stage — how many cats live in the cattery. The felinology club standard is 3 square meters per cat. A cattery with 20–30 cats is a serious flag: the more cats, the less attention each kitten gets, the worse the socialization, the higher the chance the kitten arrives sick (a large cat population is hard to keep in absolute cleanliness).

Another warning signal: a queen producing more than 2 litters a year. Club standard — a maximum of 1.5 litters per queen per year is registered. If a cattery shows a constant stream of kittens, ask for documents on each: irresponsible breeders exhaust queens with 3–4 litters, and on the third and fourth they simply don't issue documents (the club won't register them). The buyer doesn't know — they get a kitten with no real lineage. So check not only that the breeder is "official," but that the specific kitten has a metrika from the registry.

**What if I'm choosing between two breeders in the same price range?**

Compare documents first: [registry verification](/en/blog/how-to-verify-cattery/), publicly available genetic test results, clear contracts with sterilization clauses for pet class, and whether the breeder is asking you any questions at all. A breeder who doesn't filter buyers isn't a safer choice. They're just less thorough.
